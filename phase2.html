PHASE 2

Concerning traditional Chinese music culture, phase one evidenced what do people globally look for and why. The last steps of the research, instead, investigate its complementary aspect of online cultural consumption: what do people find while searching? Data collection categorizes the first ten websites' results per instrument, respectively, on Google and Baidu.

Visualizations evidence how each instrument's predominant categories mirror the instrument's nature and popularity. This step, beyond the subject it revolves, invites to reflect on how online cultural fruition interconnects with online cultural consumption, affecting it and being affected by it simultaneously.

INSIGHTS

Google and Baidu's first graphs comparison shows how instruments categories associations vary from inside and outside China. Wiki websites and e-commerce appear to be the most common results between traditional instruments on both sides, with Daguangxian as the most associated with wiki categories. Bawu is the most commercial instrument outside China, while it occupies only Baidu's fifth position. Zhuihu is the less commercialized instrument on Google research - with one e-commerce result out of ten - instead, for China's search engine, half of its findings are shopping related. Interesting is the fact that on both sides, Sibao is the most unpopular instrument, with almost all not-related results.

The same considerations are also visible in the visualization below, where research results are structured considering each instrument. In this diagram, Google results are divided into two parts allowing comparisons between those names recognized as "instruments" in Google Trends categories and others. The number of not-related results visually stands out in the second part, hand in hand with Google books, while e-commerce and video sharing almost disappear. Thus, it is possible to conclude that the second group is composed of instruments belonging to ancient Chinese dynasties or small ethnic groups that became unpopular over time and are not in use anymore, especially outside China.
